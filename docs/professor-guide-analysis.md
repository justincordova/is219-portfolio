# IS219 Portfolio Guide — Professor's Requirements

Source: [Web Presence Workshop Tour](https://kaw393939.github.io/designsystem/tour/)

---

## Overview

The professor's guide is a 6-step framework called **Signal → Publish**. It teaches how to build a personal portfolio that communicates deliberately — not just looks nice. Each step builds on the previous one, and the final publish step has hard requirements the site must meet.

The core philosophy: every element on the page (headline, proof, visuals, CTA) must point in the same direction, serve one audience, and survive a 5-second first glance.

---

## Step 1: Signal — Figure Out Who It's For

### What It Teaches

Before touching any code, define three things:

1. **One real person** — not a giant category like "employers" or "people in tech." A specific person in a specific situation (e.g., "A startup founder hiring their second engineer who needs evidence of fast building and clear communication").
2. **Their problem** — What problem does that person have right now, and why would a weak first impression hurt?
3. **Your promise** — What should that person understand about you immediately, in plain language.

### Why It Matters

First impressions form in under 50 milliseconds — visitors decide credibility before reading a single word. A clear signal reduces cognitive load by answering one question: "Is this for me?" The "micro-yes" happens when the right visitor recognizes themselves in your signal.

### Portfolio Prompts

- **Audience:** Name one real person in one real situation.
- **Need:** What problem does that person have and why does a weak first impression hurt?
- **Promise:** What should the portfolio make that person understand right away?

### Common Mistake

Using vague categories like "people in tech" or "employers." That is too broad to guide a real first screen.

### Quick Check

Ask another person who they think the page is for and what problem it solves. If they cannot answer both, the brief is still too vague.

## Step 2: Archetype — Pick the Vibe

### What It Teaches

Choose one brand personality (archetype) that your content and proof can actually support. This is a fit check, not a personality quiz.

### Key Archetypes

| Archetype | Core Signal | Gift | Trap | Best Fit |
|-----------|------------|------|------|----------|
| **Sage** | "This will be accurate, grounded, and worth trusting" | Deep trust because method is visible before the claim | Can feel cold or intimidating if no human warmth | Research-heavy work, technical expertise, teaching, consulting |
| **Hero** | "This will show you what is possible with effort and skill" | Inspires belief that work can deliver under pressure | Feels exhausting or self-congratulatory if proof cannot carry the energy | Achievement-oriented portfolios, results-driven work, competitive fields |
| **Caregiver** | "This is here for you" | Emotional safety, removes friction from trust | Feels paternalistic or empty if warmth is not backed by substance | Education, mentorship, healthcare, support services |

### Fit Rubric (6 Checks)

Before locking in an archetype, score it against:

1. **Audience fit** — Does this vibe match what the audience needs to feel first?
2. **Proof fit** — Do you have real artifacts that can naturally support this vibe?
3. **Tone fit** — Can the headline, rhythm, and vocabulary honestly carry this vibe?
4. **Real promise** — Does this fit what the page is really promising beyond surface style?
5. **Misuse risk** — What is most likely to feel false, shallow, or overperformed?
6. **Outside-the-page fit** — Will the same vibe make sense in posts, follow-ups, and public talk?

### Psychology Behind It

- Archetypes are universal recognition patterns (Carl Jung) — audiences recognize the personality instantly when it matches.
- Brand congruence: when archetype, visual design, tone, and proof all point the same direction, the page feels coherent. Any mismatch is sensed unconsciously.
- Every archetype has a shadow (failure mode): Sage becomes pedantic, Hero becomes arrogant, Caregiver becomes smothering.

### Quick Check

Show someone the first screen for 5 seconds. If they do not describe the same vibe you intended, the choice is not locked yet.

## Step 3: Style — Choose the Look

### What It Teaches

Pick one visual direction that reinforces the archetype. Style is how the message shows up on the page — it should sharpen the read, not fight it.

### Visual Directions

| Direction | Description | Best For | Watch Out |
|-----------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Swiss Grid** | Clarity first. Ordered, exact, quiet, fast to scan. | Sage, Ruler, Hero, some Creator brands | Can become cold, generic, or over-corporate |
| **Brutalist Interface** | Blunt, raw, unapologetic. Speaks with force, refuses polish as main value. | Hero, Outlaw, Ruler brands | Can feel hostile or unreadable if content is already demanding |
| **Punk and Grunge** | Rule-breaking, expressive, attitude-driven. Design carries edge before polish. | Outlaw, Jester, some Explorer and Creator brands | Edge must serve a real idea — if content is thin, roughness reads like theater |
| **Editorial Luxury** | Taste, pacing, and attention quality become proof. Composed, not assembled. | Lover, Creator, Ruler, some Caregiver brands | Can drift into decorative emptiness if no argument or receipts |

### Translation Checklist

A real style choice should change four things:

1. **Type** — Headline font carries the vibe; paragraph text stays easy to scan.
2. **Hierarchy** — The first screen proves what matters before it starts decorating.
3. **Imagery** — Images support the same promise as the headline, not a second mood.
4. **What to avoid** — Name directions that would be wrong so the build has real constraints.

### Design Principles That Persuade

- **Reciprocity** — Give before you ask. Lead with valuable content above the fold; put the ask after the gift.
- **Commitment & Consistency** — Small yeses build big ones. Start with a low-friction opener; let the CTA arrive as a natural next step.
- **Liking** — Mirror the audience's identity. If the style impresses designers but alienates the actual visitor, change it.

### Quick Check

Name the chosen direction and two directions that would be wrong. If you cannot rule out the wrong ones, the choice is still too fuzzy.

## Step 4: Proof — Add Evidence Near the Claim

### What It Teaches

Proof is evidence that sits near the promise. It is what makes someone believe the page fast enough to keep reading. Without it, the page is just claims.

### What a Proof Block Needs (Portfolio)

- Block title naming what the proof demonstrates
- The artifact itself: project, system, page, or piece of work
- What changed because of it, in plain language
- A method note describing the judgment or process move that mattered
- A caption tying the proof directly back to the promise

### Three Proof Principles

1. **The first read happens before anyone reads a word** — visitors classify the page from visual feel before processing the headline.
2. **Hierarchy is trust** — clear hierarchy shows someone made real decisions about what matters.
3. **Proof must stay close to the promise** — if evidence is several scrolls away from the claim, visitors leave before the page becomes believable.

### What Counts as Evidence

| Type | How It Works | Best Practice |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Social proof** | Others like me chose this. Testimonials, user counts, badges. | Use testimonials from people who match your audience, not just impressive names. Peer proof beats celebrity proof. |
| **Authority** | Endorsed by a credible source. Credentials, publications, institutional backing. | Show credentials near the claim they support. |
| **Evidence tiers** | Anecdotal → statistical → experimental. Stronger evidence goes closer to the main claim. | Audit your proof: place strongest evidence nearest to biggest promise. |

### Call to Action

The next step (CTA) should sound like the same person the page just introduced. Keep it in the same voice.

### Common Mistake

Swapping adjectives in for proof. If the page says "trusted" or "innovative" but shows nothing real nearby, people will not buy it.

### Quick Check

Point to the evidence closest to the main claim and explain why it has to be there instead of buried three scrolls later.

## Step 5: Build — Turn Decisions Into a Page

### What It Teaches

Audit what you have, pick the biggest fix, and write a brief clear enough for a classmate or AI tool to follow.

### Portfolio Audit Checklist

- [ ] Hero image or top visual
- [ ] Headline
- [ ] Supporting copy in the first two or three sentences
- [ ] First proof element visible without scrolling
- [ ] Call to action
- [ ] Overall first-read feeling

### Build Brief Checklist (Minimum Pieces)

- [ ] Completed signal brief for the portfolio
- [ ] Page audit scores
- [ ] At least one proof block drafted
- [ ] A realistic three-week build plan
- [ ] A clear idea of what to spec for AI tools this week

### Studio Review Criteria

**Both products:**
- Signal clarity: a classmate can name the archetype from a 5-second look
- Proof placement: real evidence appears before or near the main promise
- Coherence: headline, image, layout, and CTA all belong to the same archetype
- Spec quality: the student can explain what they asked the agent to build and why

**Portfolio-specific:**
- Audience fit: the page reads like it was made for one specific person rather than everyone
- Professional promise: the one-sentence promise can be stated clearly from memory

### Writing Briefs for AI Tools

An effective AI brief has three parts:
1. **Context** — what the project is and who it is for
2. **Constraints** — what to avoid, what style to match, what standards to meet
3. **Deliverables** — what the output should look like

Replace every adjective with a concrete reference: an archetype name, a color, a layout rule, or an example page.

### Key Rule

One important fix beats five vague goals.

### Quick Check

Hand the brief to someone else. If they cannot explain the page structure, proof placement, and next move without extra help, it is not ready.

## Step 6: Publish — Put It Live

### What It Teaches

Publishing is part of the process, not a bonus round. The live version is what tells you what the page actually communicates.

### Hard Requirements for Portfolio Site

> These are the explicit "Must Do" items from the guide.

- [ ] **Communicate one clear archetype on the first read**
- [ ] **State a specific professional promise on the first screen**
- [ ] **Show real proof near the promise instead of burying it**
- [ ] **Give the visitor a clear next step**
- [ ] **Feel intentionally made rather than template-shaped**

### Three-Week Publishing Schedule

| Week | Focus |
|------|-------|
| **Week 1** | Lock signal and first read. Apply signal brief to page structure and headline. |
| **Week 2** | Strengthen proof and editorial care. Add or strengthen proof blocks and tighten the CTA. |
| **Week 3** | Coherence pass and public readiness. Final polish, confirm the site can travel publicly without reading like a draft. |

### Strong Work vs. Weak Work

**Signs of strong work:**
- The goal of the page is clear and easy to say out loud
- The site is honest about what it is and who it is for
- The proof is real and easy to spot
- The build prompts get more specific over time, not more vague
- The student can explain the choices instead of only showing the final screen

**Common portfolio mistakes:**
- Mixing archetypes so the first read communicates multiple personalities at once
- Hiding the best work or strongest proof far below the fold
- Writing a headline that says nothing specific about the person or the promise
- Using a generic CTA with no reason for the visitor to act now

### Pre-Launch Verification

Before going live, verify:
1. **Accessibility** — screen reader, keyboard, contrast
2. **Performance** — loads in under 3 seconds on mobile
3. **Content matches brief** — headline, proof, CTA all present and consistent

### Publish Rule

It only works if the same vibe survives in public. The page, the spoken opener, and the follow-up piece should all sound like the same person making the same promise.
